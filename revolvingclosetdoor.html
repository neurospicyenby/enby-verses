Revolving Closet Door

Published on 14th December 2024


“When did you come out?”
That’s what they ask.
Like it’s a one-time event,
like it’s a milestone you check off,
a box you tick,
a celebration,
a declaration,
and then it’s done.

But let me tell you something:
I didn’t come out once.
I come out every bleeding day.
Because the closet isn’t a door you walk through.
It’s a revolving one.

I came out to myself first,
and that was hard enough
learning to say it without flinching,
without choking on the shame
this world handed me
before I even knew what it meant.

I came out to my friends,
testing the words on my tongue,
watching their faces,
bracing for their reactions
would they stay?
Would they leave?
Would they see me differently?

I came out to my family,
sitting through awkward silences,
tight smiles,
and the endless questions:
“Are you sure?”
“What about kids?”
“What will people think?”

But it didn’t end there.
It never does.

I come out every time someone asks,
“Do you have a girlfriend?”
and I have to decide,
in that split second,
if it’s worth correcting them
if I have the energy,
if I feel safe.

I come out at work,
when colleagues ask about my weekend,
and I hesitate,
just for a moment,
because one wrong word
“we,”
“he,”
“they”
can shift the entire room.

I come out when I hold someone’s hand
in public,
and I feel the stares,
the judgment,
the hate.
When I have to ask myself,
every single time,
“Is this worth the risk?”

And here’s the part that makes me angry:
it never stops.
Every time, there’s that pause.
That moment where I have to weigh the cost.
That moment where I have to decide
how much of myself I’m willing to show
and how much I have to hide.
And even if I’ve done it a hundred times,
a thousand times,
there’s always a risk
that this time,
this person,
this situation
will be the one that burns me.

So when you ask me,
“When did you come out?”
I want to say:
Which time?
Because this isn’t a finish line.
This isn’t a celebration.
This is a cycle
an endless, spinning, exhausting cycle.
For us,
the closet isn’t a door.
It’s a revolving trap,
and we’re forced to step through it
again and again and again.

And maybe the real question is:
Why should we have to?

Jayden C
